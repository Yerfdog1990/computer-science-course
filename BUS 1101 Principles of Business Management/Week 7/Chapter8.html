8.1: Organizational Culture

Organizational culture is a shared system of assumptions, values, and beliefs that dictates appropriate and inappropriate behaviors. It gained prominence in the 1980s, with research highlighting its connection to organizational success.

Why Organizational Culture Matters

  • Competitive Advantage: A unique and difficult-to-imitate culture can provide a significant competitive edge. For a company like Google, its culture of innovation is a key differentiator.
  • Performance: A strong culture is linked to improved performance, but it must align with the industry. An innovative culture is beneficial for a high-tech company, while a stable culture is perfect for a utility company.
  • Control Mechanism: Culture is a powerful control mechanism, often more effective than formal rules. For example, a "customer service" culture empowers employees to waive a refund fee to satisfy a customer without a specific policy.

Levels of Organizational Culture

  • Assumptions: The deepest, unconscious beliefs about human nature.

    Example: A company's unspoken assumption that "people are lazy and need to be monitored" influences all other cultural aspects.

  • Values: Shared principles and goals that stem from assumptions.

    Example: The assumption above leads to **values** of strict accountability and formal procedures.

  • Artifacts: Visible and tangible manifestations of the culture.

    Example: The **artifacts** are a rigid dress code, clocking in and out, and performance reviews based on hours worked.

8.2: Case in Point: Google Creates Unique Culture

User Focus

Google's mission drives its emphasis on user experience, leading to a clean search page without distractions, prioritizing user needs over immediate advertising revenue.

Employee Well-being and Perks

Google offers extensive benefits like free gourmet meals, gyms, and on-site childcare, making employees feel valued and fostering a unique work environment.

Risk-Taking and Innovation

The company encourages risk. When a VP made a multi-million dollar mistake, Larry Page commended him. Engineers are also encouraged to spend 20% of their time on personal projects.

Team-Oriented Decision Making

Decisions are made in teams and through consensus. Employees are encouraged to use data and rational persuasion rather than personal opinions, a practice facilitated by open office environments.

Rigorous Hiring

Google maintains its values through an extremely competitive hiring process. Candidates are selected to ensure they align with the company's values of risk-taking, agility, and cooperation.

8.6: Creating Culture Change

Changing a deeply embedded culture is difficult but essential for survival. Here are six steps to facilitate this change.

  1. Create a Sense of Urgency: Employees must understand the critical need for change.

    Example: Lou Gerstner used the crisis at IBM in 1993 to gain buy-in for a cultural transformation by convincing employees the company's existence was at stake.

  2. Change Leaders and Other Key Players: Leadership must champion the new culture.

    Example: Robert Iger, as Disney CEO, abolished a central planning unit that was a barrier to creativity, removing a key roadblock to an innovative culture.

  3. Role Modeling: Leaders should exhibit the behaviors expected of employees.

    Example: Iger personally engaged in game creation at Disney to model his commitment to innovation.

  4. Training: Structured programs can instill new norms.

    Example: After the Columbia disaster, NASA implemented training in team processes and cognitive bias awareness to foster a safety-sensitive culture.

  5. Changing the Reward System: Rewards and punishments must align with new values.

    Example: Shifting from individual commissions to team-based bonuses can promote collaboration.

  6. Creating New Symbols and Stories: New rituals, symbols, and narratives reinforce cultural shifts.

    Example: Continental Airlines famously burned its 800-page policy manual to symbolize a move toward a less bureaucratic culture.

8.7: Developing Your Personal Skills: Learning to Fit In

This section provides practical guidance for individuals to understand and adapt to an organizational culture, both before and after joining a company.

Before You Join

  • Do Your Research: Investigate the company through friends, news, and its website to get a sense of its values.
  • Observe the Physical Environment: Note the office layout, dress code, and employee demeanor during a visit.
  • Read Between the Lines: The absence of a lengthy employee handbook might suggest a more flexible culture.
  • Reflect on How You Are Treated: The recruitment process itself can reveal the company's respect for candidates.
  • Ask Questions: Inquire about what leads to success in the firm.
  • Listen to Your Gut: Trust your instincts about the overall environment, manager, and coworkers.

After You Get a New Job (Onboarding)

  • Gather Information: Observe interactions, dress, and behaviors to understand the company and your role.
  • Manage Your First Impression: Dress appropriately, be friendly, and express enthusiasm.
  • Invest in Relationship Development: Build relationships with your manager and coworkers by initiating conversations.
  • Seek Feedback: Regularly ask for feedback on your performance and act on suggestions for improvement.
  • Show Success Early On: Volunteer for high-profile projects to demonstrate your skills and meet key people.
