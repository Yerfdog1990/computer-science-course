General Status

  • Throughout human history, women have frequently been relegated to a secondary social class, sometimes even considered *de facto* slaves in the most oppressive and basic societies.
  • Despite this historical trend, women in more advanced civilizations like ancient Rome were able to hold social stations of varying importance, which is often seen as a mark of high culture.
  • However, it can also be argued that these types of civilizations, including Rome, clung to the practice of gender-based discrimination long after they had grown beyond any pragmatic or logical reason for that kind of social division.

Women Under Roman Law

  • Roman laws regarding women‚Äôs legal standing underwent changes over the course of ancient Rome‚Äôs existence, but freeborn women were recognized as legal Roman citizens as early as the days of the Early Roman Republic in the 5th century BC.
  • This citizenship granted Roman women significant legal rights that were almost unprecedented in the ancient world. They were able to own land, represent themselves in legal matters, and sign official documents.
  • Furthermore, in matters of inheritance and estate, women were equally considered alongside any male heir unless a will specifically dictated otherwise. This provided a level of financial autonomy rare for women in antiquity.
  • Despite these somewhat enlightened policies, Roman women were still firmly second-class citizens. They were explicitly prohibited from voting in elections or holding publicly elected offices in government.
  • In fact, every woman in Roman society was politically and legally subordinate to a close male relative called a *pater familias*, or head of household, who held official authority over them. This position would typically be held first by a girl‚Äôs father (or an older brother or uncle in the event of the father‚Äôs death) and would then be transferred to her husband after she was married.
  • A woman‚Äôs social class was entirely determined by the social class of her *pater familias*.
  • This arrangement was largely due to assertive actions on the part of those in the male-dominated power structure, who firmly believed women were suitable only for elegance and finery and had no place or capacity in the political sphere.
  • Despite these significant legal and political restrictions, it was still possible for Roman women of high social standing to have an indirect but real impact on public policy and even military matters. If a woman happened to be the daughter or wife of an influential official or general, that male relative might be willing to listen to their counsel and opinions.
  • There are a few select examples of women in Rome who attained a very high level of real political power through this method. However, this influential role was still entirely dependent on having that type of male relative and was not legally recognized in any way. Furthermore, only a very small number of Roman women were part of the elite families who could express this type of pseudo-authority.
  • One unique societal role that was reserved exclusively for women was that of the Vestal Virgins. These women were priestesses of Vesta, the Roman goddess of the hearth. They took 30-year vows of celibacy, which freed them from the traditional child-bearing obligations expected of Roman women.
  • The Vestal Virgins were responsible for studying religion and the dogma of Roman politics to ensure that all rituals were performed properly; these were topics that were explicitly off-limits to the male college of priests. The Vestal Virgins were highly important in Roman society, and their continued existence and purity were seen as fundamental to Rome‚Äôs longevity and security (it was believed that if the sacred flame they tended went out, Rome would suffer misfortune).
üëßüë¶ Childhood & Education
  • A young woman in ancient Rome would typically have a very different childhood experience compared to her male counterparts, shaped by distinct societal expectations.
  • In their earliest years, children of both genders played with toys and dolls that were similar to modern ones, indicating a universal aspect of early childhood.
  • From there, each would enter some form of schooling. However, boys would learn about rhetoric and history, preparing them for public life, while girls learned skills that were more suited to their expected existence working primarily in the home.
  • Since Roman girls were often married as young as age 12, their formal schooling was also considerably shorter than a boy‚Äôs, reflecting their earlier transition into adult domestic roles.
  • As with many aspects of Roman society, those in the higher social classes had a different experience. Girls from wealthy families could be privately tutored in many different subjects, even after they were married and had children, allowing for continued intellectual development.
  • Interestingly, there was no general misogynistic preference for sons as existed in many other ancient societies, save perhaps for emperors who specifically desired a son to inherit the imperial title. Any Roman family would be just as proud to present their daughters to visitors and at events as they would be their sons.
  • Young women were also expected to learn and work hard in order to excel in their expected roles as managers of domestic affairs. Rather than being cast aside or relegated to this position as inferior, women were seen as respected members of society who fulfilled a set of highly important and necessary functions within the household and family economy.

Emphasis on Virginity and Sexuality

  • Like many civilizations throughout history, ancient Roman society was highly concerned with a woman‚Äôs virginity and general sexuality. The virginity of young women was especially protected, and sexual relationships prior to official marriage were heavily discouraged.
  • This emphasis on virginity ran so deep in ancient Rome that it was not unheard of for a young woman to be executed for engaging in sexual relations before marriage, highlighting the extreme societal value placed on premarital purity.
  • This extreme stance was even reflected in their religion with the existence of Pudicitia‚Äîthe Roman goddess of feminine purity‚Äîwho could only be worshiped by virginal women. Similarly, the ecclesiastical order of the Vestal Virgins, whose non-sexual status was thought to give them special insight into religious traditions, underscored this societal value.

Types of Marriage

  • Marriages were fundamental institutions in Roman society, with several distinct forms:
    • *Coemptio*: This was essentially a business transaction, where a father symbolically sold his daughter into marriage, reflecting a transfer of control.
    • *Confarreatio*: A much more formal and modern form of marriage, which involved a ceremony in front of witnesses and was quite similar to marriage in our own present-day culture. It was mostly used by the social elite and even included the Roman equivalent of a wedding cake, signifying its importance and formality.
    • *Usus*: This was a relatively informal agreement between a man and woman to cohabitate and raise children together. After they had lived together continuously for one year, the woman passed under the control of her husband in a *manus* marriage. This was probably the kind of marriage most typical among ordinary or poorer Romans due to its simplicity.
    • Concubinage: This was an official form of pseudo-marriage where a woman would live with a man and be his sexual partner, but was technically not his wife. This was usually done when the woman was of a lower social class than the man, making legal marriage an impossibility due to social conventions. That being said, there was no particular stigma associated with a woman becoming a concubine, or for a man living with one.

Marital Expectations and Fidelity

  • Regardless of the type of marriage, all reputable women were expected to begin their sexual lives on the night of their wedding in their husband‚Äôs bedroom.
  • Furthermore, wives were strictly expected to engage in sexual activity with no one other than their husbands. In stark contrast, men were free to have extramarital sex with multiple women, provided that those women were prostitutes or slaves, highlighting a significant double standard.
  • This societal focus on a woman‚Äôs marital fidelity often bordered on obsession, with many prominent Roman men assigning minders to their wives to prevent potential clandestine encounters with other men.
  • Julius Caesar himself famously divorced his second wife, Pompeia, at the mere suspicion that she may be capable of adultery, demonstrating the severe consequences of even perceived infidelity.

Divorce

  • When it came to divorce, ancient Rome had relatively progressive public policies compared to many other ancient societies. Both women and men were free to request a divorce, and it was often an informal matter without significant shame attached to it.
  • Adultery was a very common motive for divorce. Women were treated quite harshly in the event of infidelity, even facing banishment from Rome under the rule of Augustus during early Imperial Rome.
  • Meanwhile, men were only guilty of adultery under Roman law if their sexual partner was either married to another Roman citizen or was the unmarried daughter of one, further illustrating the gendered nature of the law.
  • Following a divorce, remarriage was fairly common, particularly among the political and economic elite, who often married for pragmatic reasons (e.g., alliances, wealth) rather than love.
üè° Daily Life & Domestic Role

Traditional Domestic Role

  • Traditionally speaking, a woman‚Äôs primary and most important role was to maintain the domestic realm of the household, overseeing all aspects of home life.
  • For members of the lower social classes, this consisted primarily in making clothing for the rest of the family. In fact, the ability for a household to combine all of its labor to raise sheep and use their wool to make clothing was a significant mark of self-sufficiency and economic independence in ancient Roman culture.
  • In wealthy and powerful Roman families, this female role as head of domestic affairs could become incredibly complicated and demanding, often comparable to running a large corporation. These families frequently owned multiple households or estates, each with its own incomes, large staff of servants, and important logistical considerations that required careful management.
  • In some cases, powerful women were able to wield this influence effectively in order to secure properties or incomes for their husbands that would have otherwise been lost, demonstrating their significant practical impact.

Financial Independence and Business Ventures

  • One unique and progressive aspect of Roman marriage was that money and property were not automatically shared between spouses. Instead, each member of the marriage maintained their own separate assets.
  • This legal framework meant that women often had their own money to launch or invest in business ventures, which they frequently did, showcasing their entrepreneurial spirit.
  • There are many documented instances of Roman women owning businesses like brickworks and metalsmiths, indicating their active participation in various industries. Their access to these industries was aided by the fact that Roman society considered many jobs of industry (e.g., manufacturing, trade) to be beneath the wealthiest and most socially elevated of Rome‚Äôs citizens, keeping men out of those fields entirely.
  • Even women who did not possess enough personal wealth to fund their own businesses often helped with their husband's enterprises, both in administrative roles and by performing some of the physical work themselves.
  • Another common and respected job for women in ancient Rome was as wet nurses or midwives for other women, providing essential healthcare services.
  • However, occupations such as actresses, dancers, and prostitutes were associated with a permanent downgrade to the woman‚Äôs social status, highlighting societal judgments based on profession.

Motherhood and Child-Rearing

  • Roman women were generally expected to become mothers after they were married, as procreation was a central purpose of marriage.
  • Women who were unmarried or could not have children faced significant social and legal disadvantages; they were prohibited from inheriting property by traditional means, except when they were specifically named in someone‚Äôs will.
  • As part of their roles as managing the domestic sphere, many Roman women were their child‚Äôs primary caregivers, although there is evidence that fathers sometimes shared in these responsibilities, indicating some variation in practice.
  • That being said, women who were more affluent and could afford to hire wet nurses and servants to shoulder child-rearing duties often did so in order to free themselves up to pursue social or business ambitions. There was no stigma attached to this practice, and it was a common and accepted norm among Rome‚Äôs elite families.
üíî Prostitution & Slavery

Prostitution

  • Despite ancient Rome‚Äôs strong societal predilection towards female chastity and perceived feminine purity, prostitution was incredibly prolific throughout Rome and its territories.
  • Any woman who chose to become a prostitute to support herself financially was legally free to do so. This was most common among women who were freed slaves, highly impoverished, or who no longer had a *pater familias* to be responsible for them, but there were also documented cases of high-class women engaging in prostitution.
  • During the Imperial Roman era, women who were found guilty of crimes could be forced into prostitution as a form of punishment, although this practice eventually ceased in 389 AD when it was outlawed.
  • Regardless of who the woman was or how she ended up as a prostitute, engaging in the practice would create a permanent demotion in her social class. Women who had previously engaged in prostitution were barred from certain social institutions and prohibited from legally marrying most Roman men who did not have similarly marred reputations, instead being relegated to concubinage.

Slavery and Enslaved Women

  • One aspect of ancient Rome that is often overlooked is the widespread prevalence of slavery in their society. As Rome conquered more and more of the Mediterranean world, non-combatants living in defeated kingdoms were rounded up and enslaved wholesale.
  • The overwhelming majority of these enslaved captives were women, who became ubiquitous in the city of Rome, forming a significant portion of the enslaved population.
  • Since slaves had no legal rights and were treated like any other item of property, they were frequently forced into prostitution by their owners.
  • However, a slave owner could place a *ne serva* clause onto the slave‚Äôs ownership terms, which would immediately emancipate the slave if they were ever forced into prostitution, offering a rare form of protection.
  • While rape was legally considered a crime in ancient Rome, the law did not apply to slaves. It was only illegal to assault an enslaved person if the slave was seriously injured, because that would amount to property damage to the owner's property, not a crime against the person.
üåü Prominent Roman Women
  • Lucretia: A semi-mythological noblewoman who lived during the last years of the Roman Kingdom. According to legend, her alleged rape by the king‚Äôs son, Sextus, was an extreme example of royal abuse of power. Lucretia's revelation to her family and powerful friends sparked a popular uprising that ultimately overthrew the monarchy and established the Roman Republic in 509 BC.
  • Cornelia: Born during the Late Roman Republic to Publius Cornelius Scipio Africanus, a prominent Roman general. She married into the Roman aristocracy and became highly educated in literature, writing, mathematics, and politics. She famously tutored her sons, Tiberius and Gaius, known as the Gracchi brothers. Both served as Tribunes of the Plebs and advocated for radical popular reforms. Cornelia counseled them throughout their political careers, and there is evidence that she significantly influenced their shared ideology.
  • Aurelia: The mother of the legendary Roman general and dictator, Gaius Julius Caesar. Already a member of an elite aristocratic family herself, Aurelia raised Caesar and actively fostered his ambitions for greatness. When he faced a crucial trial for defying an order, Aurelia was one of the key people who successfully testified on his behalf at the hearings. She remained a member of Caesar‚Äôs inner court throughout his reign, reportedly helping her son maintain control of power by alerting him to potential betrayals.
  • Fulvia: A central figure in the highly dramatic whirlwind of events that led to the assassination of Julius Caesar and the death of the Roman Republic. Fulvia attained an impressive level of wealth and political influence by marrying and being widowed by a series of powerful Roman men, eventually marrying the general Mark Antony. After Caesar was killed, Antony took power but left in pursuit of Caesar's assassins, effectively leaving Fulvia as the *de facto* ruler of Rome. She marshaled eight legions to support her, but Antony famously became entangled with Cleopatra, and Fulvia‚Äôs forces were ultimately defeated by Octavian, the future Emperor Augustus of Rome.
  • Livia Drusilla: A prominent Roman empress, the wife of Emperor Augustus, and the mother of Emperor Tiberius. Livia was renowned for her influential role in the Roman Empire, actively counseling Augustus on affairs of state and working diligently to secure the imperial succession for her son, Tiberius. She was also reputed to have caused the deaths of many of her son's rivals, including Marcus Claudius Marcellus. Livia has been both demonized as a murderous villainess and held up as an example of womanly virtue and simplicity. She was a long-lived and exceptionally powerful figure in the early years of the Roman Principate.
  • Agrippina the Elder: Born to a high-class Roman family during the early Imperial era under Emperor Augustus. She married the general Germanicus and, in a significant break from Roman tradition, traveled with him and her children on his military campaigns. She is even credited with helping to prevent a mutiny in the Roman province of Gaul. After Germanicus‚Äô death, she was boldly outspoken in her belief that her husband was secretly assassinated. Her son Gaius would later become the emperor Caligula, whom Agrippina continued to counsel until both were exiled from Rome in 29 AD.
  • Agrippina the Younger (Julia Agrippina): The daughter of Agrippina the Elder, she rose to become Empress of Rome by virtue of being married to the emperor Claudius. After his death, she skillfully used her extensive political connections to place her son-in-law, Nero, on the throne. During Nero‚Äôs reign, Agrippina used her significant influence to control matters of the Roman state like few women ever had. She was killed in 59 AD, though the exact circumstances of her death remain impossible to know given the many conflicting and clearly biased accounts from the time.
  • Valeria Messalina: The third wife of the Emperor Claudius, a man thirty years her elder. According to widespread rumors, she would seduce many men in the Roman court and form a powerful political faction that would murder her rivals with the tacit consent of the emperor, whom she was said to have "wrapped around her finger." Valeria became incredibly influential and was on her way to securing succession for her son, Britannicus. However, her ambition ultimately led to her downfall when she was discovered plotting with one of her lovers, Gaius Silius, to kill Emperor Claudius, leading to him having them both executed in 48 AD.
  • Helena: The wife of the Roman Emperor Constantius during the Late Imperial era. In 326 AD, she converted to Christianity and undertook a significant pilgrimage to the Holy Land in modern-day Israel, which was under Roman control at the time. She had churches built in important Christian sites like Bethlehem and Jerusalem and brought many relics back to Rome. Her son, the Emperor Constantine, would also convert to Christianity and famously make it the official religion of the Empire. Helena was canonized by the Catholic Church after her death for her profound achievements on behalf of Christianity.
  • Julia Mamaea: Born into a very powerful Roman family, her cousin was the emperor Caracalla, and later, her nephew was emperor Elagabalus, making her and her family a common sight in the Roman court. When Elagabalus was assassinated, her son Severus was chosen to succeed him, due largely in part to the political support Mamaea had cultivated. While Severus ruled, Mamaea had such a profound influence on Roman affairs that his enemies found it easy to criticize him for being her puppet. Eventually, members of the military became frustrated with Severus‚Äô lack of leadership and killed both him and Mamaea in 235 AD.
