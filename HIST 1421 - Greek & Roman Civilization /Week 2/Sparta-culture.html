The Spartan Social Pyramid

Spartan society was rigidly structured into distinct classes, each with specific rights and duties. The Spartiates, though citizens, were a small minority ruling over a vast population of non-citizens and state-owned serfs known as Helots. This imbalance was a source of constant tension and heavily influenced Spartan life and policy. Explore the chart and cards to see how each group fit into the whole.

Spartiates

Full citizens, the military elite.

The ruling class, required to undergo rigorous military training (agoge) and dedicate their lives to the state. They were forbidden from engaging in agriculture or trade, focusing solely on warfare.

Perioikoi

"Dwellers around," free non-citizens.

They handled Sparta's commerce and crafts, work forbidden to Spartiates. While they had no political rights in Sparta, they were autonomous in their own settlements and served in the army.

Helots

State-owned serfs, the majority population.

Tied to the land, they worked as farmers and laborers, turning over half their produce to their Spartiate masters. Though enslaved, they could marry and own some property, but lived under constant threat from the state.

Mothakes

Free non-Spartans raised as Spartans.

Often the illegitimate sons of Spartiate fathers and Helot mothers, they underwent the agoge alongside Spartiates. They could achieve distinction in battle but rarely full citizenship.

The Spartan Woman: A Class Apart

In a world where women were often confined and uneducated, Spartan women experienced a level of freedom and respect that was unique in Classical Greece. Their primary role was to bear strong children for the state, a duty which afforded them significant status and influence. The differences between a Spartan woman's life and that of her counterparts in cities like Athens were stark and profound.

Spartan Women

  • Educated: Received formal education in reading, writing, music, and dance.
  • Physically Active: Fed the same as boys and encouraged to exercise and compete in sports to ensure they were fit for childbearing.
  • Late Marriage: Married in their late teens or early 20s, improving health and survival rates during childbirth.
  • Social Freedom: Moved freely in public, wore practical clothing, and managed estates while men were at war.
  • Economic Power: Could inherit and own property, giving them significant economic influence.

Athenian Women (for comparison)

  • Uneducated: Generally received no formal education and were taught only domestic skills.
  • Secluded: Confined to the home and rarely seen in public. Their lives were spent indoors.
  • Early Marriage: Married as young as 12 or 13, leading to high-risk pregnancies.
  • Restricted: Wore heavy, concealing clothing and had no freedom of movement without a male escort.
  • No Economic Power: Could not own property or conduct business. They were entirely dependent on male relatives.
