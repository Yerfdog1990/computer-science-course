The Roman Conquest of Syria & Egypt

Explore how Rome expanded its empire into the strategic and wealthy regions of Syria and Egypt.

Syria: Crossroads of Ancient Civilizations

  • Syria was a strategic region, influenced by Hittites, Amorites, Egyptians, Assyrians, Babylonians, and Persians.
  • It was home to the highly advanced Phoenicians, who developed the 30-letter Semitic alphabet (basis for Greek and Western languages) and established vast trade networks and colonies (e.g., Carthage).
  • After periods of Assyrian and Babylonian control, Persia dominated Syria for three centuries (6th-4th BC), allowing considerable autonomy and fostering prosperity.

Hellenistic Dominance under the Seleucids

  • Alexander the Great conquered Syria in 333 BC, introducing significant Hellenistic cultural change.
  • After Alexander's death (323 BC), his general Seleucus established the Seleucid dynasty, ruling Syria for three centuries.
  • Under the Seleucids, Greek influence grew, new cities emerged, and trade flourished, reaching India and China. Advancements in irrigation, astronomy, and philosophy marked this period.

The Clash with Rome: The Syrian War (2nd Century BC)

  • King Antiochus III of Syria critically underestimated Rome's growing power, expanding westward despite Rome's rise after defeating Carthage.
  • Battle of Thermopylae (191 BC):
    • Antiochus's 10,000-man army met a Roman force twice its size under Consul Manius Acilius Glabrio.
    • Antiochus aimed to block Roman legions in the narrow pass, using fortifications and a phalanx, while awaiting reinforcements.
    • Marcus Porcius Cato's detachment outflanked Aetolian allies, surprising them from behind.
    • Seleucids panicked and fled, hindered by their own fortifications and difficult terrain, leading to a decisive Roman victory.
  • Roman Naval Victories (191-190 BC):
    • Rome established naval supremacy in Asia.
    • Battle of Cape Korykos (191 BC): Roman and Rhodian allies defeated Antiochus's admiral Polyxenidas using iron hooks to turn the naval battle into a hand-to-hand fight.
    • Battle of Myonnesus (190 BC): Roman fleet, aided by Rhodian allies and using fire at ship prows, outmaneuvered Syrians, breaking their formation and forcing a retreat.
  • Battle of Magnesia (190 BC):
    • Antiochus confronted Romans at Magnesia ad Sipylum, forced to fight on unfavorable ground by Lucius Cornelius Scipio.
    • Roman flexible manipular legions faced a larger but haphazard Seleucid army (heavy infantry, elephants, cataphracts, scythed chariots).
    • Scythed chariots caused chaos in fog, exploited by Roman forces.
    • Roman cavalry outflanked Seleucid left wing, causing collapse.
    • Roman skirmishers disrupted the phalanx, exposing it to flanking attacks.
    • Seleucid forces, including elephants and camels, fled in disorder, leading to a complete rout. Antiochus escaped.

Decline of Seleucid Rule and Roman Annexation

  • Defeat at Magnesia severely weakened the Seleucid dynasty, leading to internal disarray and external pressures from Nabataeans, Armenians, and the Parthian Empire.
  • By the 1st century BC, Hellenistic rule was in shambles, prompting increased Roman interference due to Mithridates VI and rampant piracy.
  • In 66 BC, Pompey the Great's campaign brought the region under Roman control. In 64 BC, Syrian kings were ousted, and Pompey officially annexed Syria as a Roman province.

Syria Under Roman Rule

  • Syria eventually prospered as a Roman province, serving as a vital frontier buffer zone and economic hub for trade with the Far East.
  • It exported grains, fruits, cloth, glass, wool, linen, textiles, pottery, timber, resin, and especially purple dye.
  • Syria remained a critical military outpost, garrisoning up to four Roman legions (e.g., Legio III Gallica, VI Ferrata, X Fretensis, XII Fulminata, IIII Scythica, XVI Flavia).
  • After the split of the Roman Empire, Syria fell under Byzantine authority but succumbed to Arab invasions in the 7th century AD, becoming an Islamic cultural center.

Egypt: A Rich Prize for Rome

  • Egypt became a Roman province after the death of Cleopatra VII in 30 BCE, ending the Ptolemaic dynasty.

Early Roman Involvement (2nd Century BCE - 48 BCE)

  • Rome's involvement in Egyptian politics began in the 2nd century BCE with Ptolemy VI.
  • Egypt experienced turmoil under Greek-speaking Ptolemaic rulers who rarely learned native Egyptian.
  • Ptolemy VI regained control with Roman assistance after Seleucid invasions.
  • Ptolemy XI (88 BCE) awarded Egypt and Cyprus to Rome to secure his throne, but was despised by Alexandrians and later expelled. He regained power through Roman ties.
  • Pompey and Caesar in Egypt (48 BCE):
    • Pompey sought refuge in Egypt after defeat by Caesar but was killed by Ptolemy VIII to gain Caesar's favor.
    • Caesar, displeased by Pompey's beheading, sided with Cleopatra VII against her brother Ptolemy VIII.
    • After Ptolemy's defeat, the Ptolemaic kingdom became a Roman client state.
    • Local traditions were strong: an accidental killing of a sacred cat by a Roman led to his execution by a mob.

Antony, Octavian, and the End of the Ptolemaic Dynasty (44-30 BCE)

  • Cleopatra allied with Mark Antony after Caesar's assassination.
  • Antony's ambition and vision of Alexandria as a new Rome drew Octavian's ire.
  • Their decisive defeat at the Battle of Actium in 31 BCE by Octavian's forces sealed their fate.
  • Cleopatra's Suicide (30 BCE):
    • Facing humiliation and capture by Octavian, Cleopatra chose suicide at age 39.
    • Tradition suggests she used venom from serpents at a temple to Isis.
    • Her suicide was a staged ritual to deny Rome the spectacle of her capture, reflecting ancient Egyptian pharaonic practices.
    • Cleopatra, an educated and disciplined monarch fluent in multiple languages, was scorned in Rome but cherished in Egypt, where efforts were made to preserve her memory.
    • Her monetary reform strengthened Egypt's economy, approximating bronze coinage value to the Roman denarius.
    • A Greek papyrus with her handwritten annotation "Make it happen!" confirms her active role in administration.

Egypt Becomes a Roman Province (30 BCE)

  • Octavian's victory secured Rome the richest Mediterranean kingdom; Egypt became the "breadbasket" and "jewel" of the Roman Empire.
  • Octavian (Augustus) took absolute personal control, treating it as his private kingdom; Senators were prohibited from visiting without permission.
  • Roman Administration:
    • Roman law superseded Egyptian traditions, but many Ptolemaic institutions remained with administrative changes.
    • Augustus filled administration with equestrian class members; a prefect governed, supported by a Nile flotilla and three legions (later reduced).
    • First governor, Cornelius Gallus, committed suicide after displeasing Augustus with "grandiose claims" in Sudan, leading to a fixed frontier.

Social & Cultural Divisions in Roman Egypt

  • Rome introduced a new social hierarchy: Hellenic residents (Greek ancestry) formed the elite, enjoying tax exemptions.
  • Non-Alexandrians were "Egyptians," mostly peasants whose food production was exported to Rome.
  • The cultural divide between Hellenic cities and Egyptian-speaking villages persisted.
  • Inter-ethnic Strife:
    • Influx of Jews and Greeks into Alexandria led to riots under Emperors Claudius (41-54 CE) and Nero (54-68 CE), requiring legions to quell.

Attitude Towards Roman Control & Later Revolts

  • Initially, Egypt accepted Roman control; Alexandria played a key role in Vespasian's ascendancy (69 CE) by securing grain shipments.
  • Growing Discontent:
    • Jewish riots in 115 CE (Babylonian Revolt/Kitos War) voiced discontent, leading to thousands of deaths.
    • Dissatisfaction became part of the Egyptian psyche, leading to recurring revolts (e.g., under Antonius Pius in 150s CE, Aurelian in 273 CE, Diocletian in 295/296 CE).
  • Major Disasters and External Invasions:
    • Antonine plague (2nd CE) disrupted control.
    • Queen Zenobia of Palmyra invaded in 270 CE, conquering Egypt, Palestine, Syria, and Mesopotamia, and cutting off Rome's corn supply. Emperor Aurelian defeated her in 271 CE.

The End of Roman Egypt

  • Emperor Diocletian divided the empire into a tetrarchy (late 3rd CE); Egypt was placed in the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire.
  • While still supplying grain to Rome, resources were diverted to Syria, and Constantinople became the new economic center.
  • Egypt remained part of the Byzantine Empire until the 7th century, when it fell under Arab control and became an Islamic cultural center.
