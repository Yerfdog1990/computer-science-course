The Roman Conquest of Italy

A detailed overview of Rome's expansion across the Italian peninsula.

üìú Introduction
  • With the expulsion of Etruscan kings and the establishment of the Republican system, Rome began its expansion.
  • By 509 BC, Rome was the dominant power in Latium, recognized by a treaty with Carthage.
  • The Latin League, an alliance of city-states, initially sought to maintain independence from Rome.
  • Rome's early expansion involved a mix of conflict and alliances to consolidate control.
  • The Battle of Lake Regillus (496 BC) demonstrated Rome's ability to stand against its neighbors.
  • The *Foedus Cassianum* (Treaty of Cassius) in 493 BC granted the Latin League independence, placing Rome on nearly equal footing with the combined League members.
  • External pressures from tribes like the Volsci, Aequi, and Sabines led to closer ties with Rome and their eventual absorption.
  • By the 390s BC, Rome and the Latins had secured control of the plains, defeating the Volsci by 377 BC and effectively eliminating the Aequi.
  • The Sabines were also gradually absorbed into Roman culture after a major Roman victory in 449 BC.
üåç The Consolidation of Latium
  • Rome became the dominant player in Latium by 509 BC, inheriting leadership from the Etruscans.
  • An early treaty with Carthage recognized Roman authority over other Latin states.
  • The Latin League, formed by city-states, aimed for mutual strength and unity against Rome.
  • The Latin League was a cooperation of states sharing common interests like religious worship and defense.
  • Latium was fertile, wealthy, and had sea access, making it an inviting target for enemies like Etruscans, Volscians, and Aequians.
  • Latin League cities shared commercial treaties and granted rights of commerce, intermarriage, and settlement to citizens.
  • These rights formed the basis of Latin rights crucial in later Roman politics.
  • After Etruscan rule broke, the League states vied for dominance, with power shifting between Rome, Alba Longa, and Lavinium.
  • In 496 BC, Lavinium broke its alliance, leading to the Battle of Lake Regillus, where Rome claimed victory over united neighbors.
  • The victory, whether decisive or a stalemate, proved Rome's ability to stand against combined forces.
  • By 493 BC, the *Foedus Cassianum* (Treaty of Cassius) granted the Latin League independence, placing Rome on nearly equal status with the combined League.
  • External pressures from Umbrians (Volsci and Aequi) and Sabines forced Rome's neighbors into closer ties, leading to absorption into the Roman sphere.
  • The 5th century BC saw constant expansion among Oscan-Umbrian hill peoples.
  • The Hernicii joined a mutual protection treaty with Romans and Latins in 486 BC due to pressure from Aequi and Volsci.
  • Hernicii were soon absorbed into Latin culture, and conflicts were almost yearly.
  • Latin towns Tibur and Praeneste were threatened by Aequi raids, some purportedly reaching Rome's gates.
  • In 458 BC, a Roman army was supposedly eliminated at Agidus Pass; Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus, appointed Dictator, won a decisive victory over the Aequi, though they soon resumed war.
  • The Volsci controlled much of southern Latium and pressured Latins, with legendary renegade Coriolanus allegedly reaching Rome.
  • The decisive battle against Umbrian invaders was in 431 BC at Algidus Pass, where Romans were victorious.
  • By the 390s BC, Romans and Latins regained control of plains, relegating Aequi and Volsci to highlands.
  • Volsci were finally defeated with the capture of Antium in 377 BC; Aequi disappeared as a separate entity around the same time.
  • Sabines constantly pressured territory between Tiber and Anio Rivers.
  • A Sabine invasion allegedly seized Rome in 460 BC, but after a major Roman victory in 449 BC, Sabines disappeared from history for two centuries, eventually absorbed into Roman culture.
  • One Sabine group, including Attus Clausus (later Gens Claudian), zealously allied with Rome and moved onto Roman territory, becoming Romans.
  • Rome's military policy became more aggressive between 449 and 390 BC after the victory over Umbrians.
  • Etruscans, especially Veii, remained a constant source of strife.
  • By 396 BC, Gallic invasions weakened Etruscans, opening the door for Roman conquest.
  • Gallic movements from the north foreshadowed disastrous conflicts.
  • Further Roman expansion south would lead to a series of wars with the Samnites.
  • After 300 years, Rome had yet to firmly secure Latium and faced significant challenges.
üèõÔ∏è Veii and the Etruscans
  • Despite removing Etruscan rule in the late 6th century BC, Etruscans remained a threat for three centuries.
  • The Etruscan city-state of Veii, 12 miles north of Rome, was a main source of concern due to its equal strength.
  • Control of the Tiber River, a vital transportation and commerce artery, was crucial for both cities.
  • Control of the Tiber granted access to Western Italy, Latium, Samnium, Etruria, and parts of northern Campania.
  • Access to the salt mines of Ostia, at the mouth of the Tiber, was also of utmost importance.
  • Early 5th century BC: Conflicts escalated into war, including the legendary destruction of 300 Fabii at the Battle of Cremera.
  • Within a year of Cremera, the Etruscan navy was destroyed by Hieron of Syracuse off Cumae, a military disaster.
  • Etruscan city-states, including Veii, became more fragmented, losing mutual protection strength.
  • Veii was forced to make a treaty with Rome after its naval defeat.
  • Celtic Gauls migrating into northern Italy from the 6th century BC further debilitated the Etruscans.
  • Between 406 and 396 BC, Rome went on the offensive against weakened Etruscans.
  • Under Marcus Furius Camillus, Fidenae was retaken, and Veii itself came under siege.
  • The legendary Siege of Veii, said to last 10 years, likely lasted less, but introduced paid professional legions.
  • The siege was reportedly broken in 396 BC when Romans drained Alban Lake, allowing soldiers to sneak under walls.
  • Camillus was credited with saving Rome and gained unending admiration.
  • Rome destroyed much of Veii, driving out residents and allotting territory to Roman citizens, creating four new tribes.
  • Veii's capture significantly increased Roman territory and strength, making Rome predominant over its divided neighbors.
  • Over the next century, Etruscan cities were slowly incorporated into Rome's fold.
  • Etruscan decline was inevitable due to their lack of unity and cooperation.
  • By 273 BC, Etruria and the Etruscans were completely within Rome's domain.
üî• The Gallic Sack of Rome
  • In the 5th and early 4th centuries BC, Germanic tribes pressured Gallic Celts to move south into northern Italy.
  • Gauls crossed the Alps, capturing and settling Etruscan territory by force.
  • Gallic tribes, united only by blood, maintained their own kings or warlords; some were aggressive.
  • One tribe, the Senones, led by Brennus, besieged the Etruscan city of Clusium.
  • Roman envoys sent to treat with Brennus escalated into violence, resulting in a Celtic chieftain's death.
  • Brennus demanded the three Roman envoys be turned over, but Rome refused.
  • The angered Gauls left Clusium and headed for Rome for revenge.
  • In July 387 BC (traditionally 390 BC), an outnumbered Roman army suffered a crushing defeat at the River Allia.
  • Roman defenders retreated to the Capitoline Hill for a siege, while civilians fled.
  • Gauls poured into Rome, slaughtering civilians, looting, and burning.
  • They attempted to attack the Capitoline, but were repulsed and never dislodged the occupants.
  • For seven months, Gauls wreaked havoc; one night assault was reportedly thwarted by sacred geese.
  • Romans engaged Brennus for terms; he agreed to leave for 1,000 lbs of gold.
  • Livy recounts Brennus's famous line, "Vae Victis" ("Woe to the vanquished!"), when Romans complained about heavy weights.
  • Romans decided to stay and quickly rebuilt the city, completing the Servian Wall.
  • As a result of the invasion, Romans adopted new military weaponry: the gladius and modified armor, abandoning Greek Phalanx spears.
  • The legion was reorganized, placing youngest and strongest soldiers in front lines.
  • The Gallic invasion weakened Rome, encouraging previously subdued Italian tribes (Etruscans, Volsci, Hernici, Aequi) to rebel.
  • Over the next fifty years, these tribes were defeated and brought back under Roman dominion.
  • Gauls continued to harass the region until a formal treaty with Rome in 345 BC, though adversarial relations persisted.
  • The sack of Rome was long remembered and avenged three and a half centuries later with Caesar's conquest of Gaul.
‚öîÔ∏è The Latin Revolt and the End of the Latin League
  • After the Gallic sack, Rome became increasingly dominant in Latium and among its Latin neighbors.
  • With the end of the First Samnite War, Rome gained control of Campania, and its people allied with Rome.
  • Latins, having aided Rome, expected equal spoils but continued to harass Samnites despite Roman peace overtures.
  • Latins and Campanians feared Rome's rising power and perceived their alliances as domination.
  • Latins demanded equal status within the league and a share in Roman governance, which was rejected.
  • Campania and Capua joined the Latins in an open revolt, known as the Latin War.
  • Rome's recent and future enemy, the Samnites, sided with Rome.
  • In 340 BC, the Latin War began with an overwhelming Roman victory at the Battle of Trifanum, led by Consul Titus Manlius Imperiosus Torquatus, with Samnite support.
  • Campanians immediately capitulated and returned to Roman control.
  • Two years of bitter fighting were needed to suppress Latin resistance.
  • In 338 BC, Rome dismantled the Latin League and came to terms with its neighbors.
  • Some Latin city-states were fully incorporated into the Republic; others received lesser Roman citizenship.
  • Rights like *Commercium* (business with Rome) and *Connubium* (inter-marriage) were granted, but only with Rome, not between other tribes.
  • The right to make alliances was reserved only for Rome, and city-states lost autonomy.
  • *Civitas sine suffragio* (citizenship without voting rights) became a standard Romanization policy for building the Empire.
  • Latin rights (*ius Latii*) served as a stepping stone for absorbed communities to gain full citizenship after accepting Roman rule.
  • Most cities no longer paid taxes but provided men for the legions.
  • Most cities retained their governing systems under Roman supervision.
  • The Latin War strengthened Rome's grip on Latium and the Italian peninsula, providing more land for settlement.
  • Rome became a centralized government, no longer just the most powerful member of an alliance.
  • Revolts by Italian communities continued for centuries, but with the Latin League's elimination, Rome became the master of central Italy.
üõ°Ô∏è The Samnite Wars
  • After surviving Gallic invasions, Rome aimed for further expansion in the mid-4th century BC.
  • Rome reconquered and absorbed Latin and Etruscan towns that had rebelled during the Gallic occupation, consolidating its position in Central Italy.
  • Romans looked south towards Campania, where Samnites had moved into fertile lands from the Appenines.
  • Samnites controlled Capua and Cumae to the south of Rome, and held sway to the east.
  • Rome entered an alliance with the Samnites in 354 BC to protect its flanks.
  • Conflict over Campanian dominance was inevitable, leading to a series of wars from 343-290 BC.
  • First Samnite War (343-341 BC):
    • War broke out near Neapolis (Naples), a Greek colony, due to Samnite invasions into Campania.
    • Neapolis appealed to Rome for assistance.
    • Roman intervention was reportedly rebuffed by Samnites, leading to war.
    • Marked by Roman victories, but cut short by a revolt of Rome's Latin neighbors.
    • Latins revolted over conscription into a Roman war outside their territory, seeing Samnite pre-occupation as an opportunity.
    • Rome broke off southern conquests to deal with the Latin revolt.
    • Resulted in the major acquisition of the rich lands of Campania, including Capua.
    • Illustrated Roman diplomacy: alliance leading to absorption of allies into Rome's dominion.
    • Campania became firmly attached to Rome, adding wealth and territory, boosting manpower for future legions.
  • Second Samnite War (327-304 BC):
    • Romans expanded into Aurunci and Sidicini territory and reasserted control of Campania.
    • Rome established a colony at Fregellae on the Liris River in 328 BC, provoking Samnites.
    • Samnites were pre-occupied with a conflict against Tarentum and King Alexander of Epirus until 331 BC.
    • Romans claimed Samnites encouraged Neapolis to expand, necessitating colonies in disputed areas.
    • Samnites garrisoned Neapolis; Roman army arrived in 327 BC, expelled garrison, starting the war.
    • Samnites controlled twice as much territory as Rome, mostly mountainous.
    • Initially favored Rome, prompting Samnium to sue for peace in 321 BC, but Rome's lopsided terms were rejected.
    • Samnites used mountainous terrain to their advantage.
    • Battle of the Claudine Forks (321 BC): Romans trapped in a mountain pass, forced to march under a "yoke of spears" in ultimate humiliation.
    • Romans reportedly gave up 600 Equites as hostages and agreed to a five-year peace, giving up Fregellae and Cales.
    • During the five-year respite (321-316 BC), Romans strengthened military position, absorbing Oufentina and Falerna tribes.
    • Romans surrounded Samnites with allies by attacking Apulia and Lucania.
    • When operations resumed in 316 BC, Rome initially lost several engagements, including Lautulae (315 BC).
    • Campania nearly rejected Rome; Romans sued for peace with some Samnite factions.
    • Samnites encouraged Etruscans to join them; Etruscans joined in 311 BC after a 40-year treaty.
    • Between 311 and 304 BC, Rome won a series of victories against both Etruscans and Samnites.
    • Etruscans forced to capitulate on severe terms in 308 BC; Samnites followed suit in 304 BC.
    • Rome gained considerable territory and established many new colonies.
    • Romans adopted the Samnite manipular military formation, more flexible than the Greek hoplite system.
    • This system evolved into the cohort formation of Roman legions.
    • Construction of Via Appia (312 BC, Rome to Capua) and Via Valeria (306 BC) facilitated military movement.
    • Final decade of 4th century BC saw resistance from Aequi, Hernici, Marsi, Marrucini, Paeligni, Frentani, and Vestini.
    • In 305 BC, Roman victory led Paeligni and Hernici to surrender.
    • Aequi defeated in 304 BC, same year Samnites sued for peace; other Central Italian tribes allied with Rome within two years.
    • Conflict with Samnites renewed within a decade.
  • Third Samnite War (298-290 BC):
    • Began in 298 BC after only six years of peace, as Samnites sought to stop Roman expansion.
    • Samnites convinced Rome's old enemies (Etruscans, Umbrians, Gauls, other regional tribes) to form a coalition.
    • Threat to Rome was real, facing coordinated enemies from all sides.
    • Early in the conflict, Rome shattered a Samnite army in the south.
    • Pivotal Battle of Sentinum (295 BC) in Umbria: more combatants than any previous Italian encounter.
    • Celtic Gauls initially pushed Romans back, but Roman military discipline crushed the coalition.
    • Samnites held out for five more years, finally capitulating in 290 BC.
    • Rome granted Samnium a favorable treaty, forcing them into an alliance and ending their unified resistance.
    • Campanian cities (Italian and Greek), including Capua, became undisputed allies with varying independence.
    • Sabines ceded territory; 13 Roman colonies settled in Campania and eastern Samnium.
    • Years surrounding Samnite Wars saw public works: Circus Maximus facelift (329 BC), Via Appia (312 BC), Aqua Appia (first Roman aqueduct, 312 BC).
    • These structures proved Rome's flourishing status even during war and expansion.
    • By end of Samnite Wars, Rome had 150,000 people (one of Mediterranean's largest cities), 1,000,000 citizens, and vast numbers obligated through Latin rights/allied status.
    • Rome was master of nearly the entire Italian peninsula, save for Gauls in the north and Greek cities like Tarentum in the south.
    • Rome's growing power attracted attention from Greece and Carthage.
üêò The Pyrrhic War
  • With the conclusion of the Samnite Wars, Rome consolidated its position as master of central Italy.
  • Rome's rising power led to inevitable conflict with Greek city-states (Magna Graecia) in southern Italy.
  • Lucanians and Bruttians attacked Greek colonies; these colonies appealed to Rome for help in 283 BC.
  • Tarentum, the most influential city of Magna Graecia, was an important Mediterranean trade hub.
  • Tarentum had the most powerful navy of any Italian city and a treaty with Rome (since 302 BC) denying Roman fleets access to its bay.
  • Rome sent troops by sea to garrison Thurii in 282 BC, aiding cities like Locri, Rhegim, Croton, and Thurii against Lucanians.
  • Tarentum considered this a treaty breach and hostile act, sinking the Roman fleet and expelling the Thurii garrison.
  • The Roman Senate sent an embassy to Tarentum to demand amends; Romans were insulted, and Rome declared war.
  • Tarentum, fearing retaliation, turned to King Pyrrhus of Epirus for assistance.
  • Pyrrhus, a distant relative of Alexander the Great, sought expansion and glory, likely aiming for Sicily.
  • Epirote armies were more modern and well-equipped than Roman counterparts.
  • In 280 BC, Pyrrhus landed in Italy with 25,000 men, including cavalry, archers, and 20 war elephants (first time seen by Italians).
  • Romans prepared to fight, encouraged by Greek cities uninterested in Pyrrhus's interference.
  • First engagement at Heraclea: Romans (50,000 men) under Publius Laverius Laevinius drove into Lucanian territory.
  • Battle of Heraclea was evenly fought; Epirote elephants drove through Roman lines, causing panic.
  • Roman army was nearly destroyed, but a wounded elephant caused panic among others, forcing Pyrrhus to call off pursuit.
  • Ancient writers suggest Roman losses of 7,000-15,000 and Epirote losses of 4,000-13,000.
  • Pyrrhus's losses were substantial; an invading army could not endure such casualties while outnumbered.
  • Pyrrhus hoped for rebellion from Rome's enemies, but only some Samnites and Lucanians joined him, showing Rome's growing stability.
  • Pyrrhus's treatment of Greek allies also turned them against him.
  • Second major conflict: Battle of Ausculum (279 BC), a massive two-day engagement in Apulia.
  • Romans used terrain to reduce effect of Epirote cavalry and elephants; first day was a draw.
  • Second day was a stalemate until elephants were brought forward; Romans' ox-drawn war wagons were overwhelmed.
  • Estimates: 6,000 Roman and 3,500 Epirote casualties; Pyrrhus commanded the field but at great cost.
  • "Pyrrhic Victory" term attributed to Ausculum; Plutarch quotes Pyrrhus: "one other such (victory) would utterly undo him."
  • Pyrrhus offered peace terms to Rome, but was refused as long as he remained in Italy.
  • Appius Claudius encouraged the Senate to refuse simple terms for Tarentum's independence.
  • Rome negotiated an alliance with Carthage, also concerned about Pyrrhus in Sicily.
  • Pyrrhus, hoping to turn Rome and Carthage against each other, became target of both.
  • In 278 BC, he sailed to Sicily to defend Greek cities, but Carthaginians were besieging Syracuse.
  • Pyrrhus evaded capture and defeated the Carthaginian army, capturing Panormus and Eryx.
  • Carthage refused his peace offer, fearing loss of Sardinia.
  • On Italian mainland, Rome pressed Tarentum; Pyrrhus sustained heavy losses in Sicily and feared entrapment.
  • Returned to Italy in 275 BC, faced Romans at Malventum (southern Italy).
  • This time, no "Pyrrhic Victory"; Epirotes defeated, retreated to Tarentum.
  • Romans learned to deal with war elephants by attacking their sides with spears, a tactic later used against Carthage.
  • Malventum was renamed Beneventum ("Good Omen") in recognition of Roman victory.
  • Pyrrhus left Italy shortly after, returning to Epirus without most of his army or gains.
  • Plutarch quotes Pyrrhus's parting words: "What a battlefield I am leaving for Carthage and Rome."
  • With Pyrrhus gone, Tarentum and other Greek cities accepted Rome's lenient peace offer.
  • Tarentum was allowed self-rule under a Roman garrison; other city-states and tribes surrendered.
  • By 272 BC, Rome was the confirmed master of the Italian peninsula.
  • Ptolemy II of Egypt confirmed Rome's power by opening a permanent embassy.
  • Roman colonies were founded all over Italy, strengthening Rome's rule.
  • Victory in the Pyrrhic War showed Rome's adaptability and change were mightier than any regional force.
  • Greek Phalanx lost dominance, replaced by adaptable Roman legions.
  • Romans' greatest strength was perseverance, continuing expansion for over half a millennium despite setbacks.
  • Pyrrhus returned to Epirus, considered a grand adventurer and great general by Hannibal.
  • Despite army destruction, he invaded Macedonia with Gallic allies, then set sights on Sparta and Argos.
  • Plutarch states Pyrrhus was killed in Argos in 272 BC when an old woman threw a tile from a roof, stunning him, leading to his beheading by a soldier.
