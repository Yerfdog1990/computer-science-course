Exploring Roman Religion

A detailed look into the beliefs, practices, and historical development of Roman religion, from ancient times to the rise of Christianity.

Nature and Significance

Roman religion was fundamentally based on **mutual trust (fides)** between god and man, rather than divine grace. Its core objective was to secure the cooperation, benevolence, and "peace of the gods" (pax deorum) to master unknown forces and live successfully. This led to the **jus divinum** ("divine law"), a body of rules dictating religious duties.

For centuries, these precepts focused almost exclusively on the **correct performance of ritual**, with little moral emphasis. Roman ceremonial was meticulously conservative, preserving very early thought. Unlike Greek religion, Roman deities were less anthropomorphic and lacked elaborate mythologies, often borrowing from Greek legends and infusing them with nationalistic stories. Roman religion had no creed, allowing freedom of thought about the gods, and generally deprecated emotion in worship.

Historical Development

Early Roman Religion

Early Romans perceived divine force (**numen**) in functions and acts, leading to "religious atomism" where countless powers were identified with specific life phases or natural phenomena.

  • Deification of Functions: A mysterious voice that once saved them in a crisis was deified as **Aius Locutius**.
  • Veneration of Objects: Awe extended to natural objects like springs, woods, and stones (possibly meteorites), and human-made items like burial places and boundary stones.
  • Sacrifice and Magic: Forces were propitiated through **sacrifice** to revitalize divinity, accompanied by prayers and sometimes coercive magic. Example: The phrase *macte esto!* ("be you increased!") was used during sacrifices. Survivals of magic were seen in rites like the **Lupercalia** festival and the dances of the **Salii** in honor of Mars. Taboos warned against dealings with strangers or lightning-struck spots.

Rome was profoundly influenced by Etruscan religious forms, characterized by deep religiosity and devotion to ritual, including the practice of **divination** to uncover divine secrets. They had a complex view of the **afterlife** and an obsession with the care for the dead.

  • Influence on Roman Religion: Romans inherited ceremonies, rituals, and temple plans (like the **Capitoline Triad: Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva**, an Etruscan institution with three chambers). The Etruscans provided Rome's first statues of gods in human shape, encouraging anthropomorphic thinking and facilitating the adoption of Greek myths. Rome's **religious calendar** also originated from this period, reconciling solar and lunar years.

Religion in the Early Republic

Rome continued to absorb Greek religious customs despite its conservatism.

  • Greek Rites Adoption: In 399 BCE, during the siege of Veii, Rome imported the Greek rite of **lectisternium**, where images of gods were displayed on couches with food, making them "welcome guests." The **supplicatio** ritual, involving public prostration, was also adopted to avert pestilences and celebrate victories. Romans also borrowed *evocatio* to invite patron deities of captured towns to migrate to Rome.

Religion in the Later Republic: Crises and New Trends

The **Second Punic War (217 BCE)** led to a surge in emotional religion and a desperate search for divine favor.

  • Introduction of New Cults: The Great Mother of Asia Minor, **Cybele** (204 BCE), and the orgiastic worship of **Dionysus (Bacchus)** spread, promising initiation and an afterlife. These **mystery religions** and Eastern astrology became immensely popular.
  • Philosophical Influence: **Stoic philosophy** (2nd-1st centuries BCE) encouraged belief in Fate and Fortune, infused spirituality by seeing the human soul as part of a universal spirit, and provided moral justification for treating all men as brothers, filling a vacuum left by formalistic state religion.

The Imperial Epoch: Final Forms of Roman Paganism

After the civil wars (30 BCE), Augustus initiated a widespread religious restoration, believing past disasters were due to neglected duties.

  • The Imperial Cult: Augustus's adoption of the name "Augustus" (reverence-worthy) and the insertion of his *numen* and *genius* into cults paved the way for his posthumous deification. Approved emperors and empresses were called *divi* (deified ones) and regarded with veneration, serving as a unifying patriotic force. Figures like Diocletian and Maximian took names like Jovius and Herculius after their patron gods.
  • Introduction of Christianity and Mithraism: New cults emerged as old religion seemed less relevant. The Persian **Mithraism** blended dualism with mystery religion initiations, popular in the army. Ultimately, **Christianity**, with its universal charity and unique blend of divinity and humanity, prevailed, becoming the official religion of the empire from 312 CE under Emperor **Constantine**.

Beliefs, Practices, and Institutions

Earliest Divinities

  • Jupiter: The sky god, akin to Greek Zeus, linked with lightning and weather.
  • Mars: A high god protecting in war and agriculture, later identified with Greek Ares and regarded as Romulus's father.
  • Quirinus: Believed to be the god Romulus became upon ascending to heaven.
  • Janus: God of doorways and archways, with no Greek equivalent; his temple gates were closed when the state was at peace.
  • Vesta: Goddess of the hearth, whose circular temple contained the eternal fire and was tended by Vestal Virgins.
  • Di Manes & Di Parentes: Collective spirits of the dead and ancestors; reverence for ancestors was central.
  • Lares & Di Penates: Originally divine ancestors, later guardians of properties/community and powers ensuring food, respectively, moving from household to state cults.

Divinities of the Later Regal Period

  • Diana: Italian wood goddess, identified with Greek Artemis; her temple on the Aventine Hill imitated a Greek model.
  • Fors Fortuna: Originally a farming deity, later represented luck, identified with Greek Tyche.
  • Capitoline Triad (Jupiter, Juno, Minerva): The most important Roman temple, an Etruscan institution influenced by Greek anthropomorphic ideas (Juno identified with Hera, Minerva with Athena). Juno supervised women's lives, Minerva concerned craftsmen.
  • Vulcan: Fire god, identified with Greek Hephaestus.
  • Saturn: Agricultural god, identified with Greek Cronus.
  • Hercules: His cult, derived from Greek Heracles, centered at the Great Altar in the cattle market, popular among traders for averting evil.

Divinities of the Republic

  • Dioscuri (Castor and Pollux): Twin horsemen of Greek origin, who helped Rome to victory at Lake Regillus and presided over annual knight parades.
  • Ceres: Old Italian deity of generative powers, identified with Greek Demeter; her cult was introduced during a famine and became a rallying ground for plebeians.
  • Apollo: Introduced from Cumae, primarily prophetic and a healer, later elevated by Augustus as patron of his regime.
  • Venus: Gained significance as the mythical mother of Aeneas, linking her to Rome's origins and the Punic Wars.
  • Deified Qualities: Divine powers representing "qualities" were worshipped, such as **Fides** ("Faith"), **Victoria** ("Victory"), **Concordia** (celebrating end of civil strife), **Salus** (health), and **Pietas** (dutifulness).

The Sun and Stars

  • Sol Indiges: The Sun god, identified with Apollo and regarded as a divine ancestor.
  • Mithra: Sun worship spread, forming a rallying point for paganism's last years, closely associated with Mithra. Emperor **Aurelian** built a magnificent temple to **Sol Invictus** ("Unconquered Sun") in Rome (274 CE).

Priests

Roman priesthoods were largely held by prominent public figures.

  • Rex Sacrorum: "King of the sacred rites," who took over religious duties after the kings' expulsion.
  • Flamines: Priests of specific cults, notably the three major flamines of Jupiter, Mars, and Quirinus. Jupiter's priest, the *flamen dialis*, was bound by archaic taboos.
  • Chief Colleges: **Pontifices** (controlled the religious system, led by the **pontifex maximus**), **Augures** (interpreted divine signs, mainly bird movements like *auspicia*), **Quindecimviri sacris faciundis** (looked after foreign rites), and **Epulones** (supervised religious feasts).
  • Fetiales: Priestly officials concerned with international relations (treaties, war declarations).
  • Vestal Virgins: Six young girls who tended Vesta's shrine and eternal fire, living under strict prehistoric taboos.

Shrines and Temples

A *templum* originally meant an area of the sky for omen interpretation, later a consecrated piece of ground.

  • Early Temples: From c. 575 BCE, including Vesta's round shrine and a group in the S. Omobono sacred area. The great Etruscan temples, made of wood with terra-cotta, culminated in the Capitoline Triad temple. Later, more solid materials like tuff, travertine, and marble were used.

Sacrifice and Burial Rites

  • Offerings: Characteristic offerings were sacrifices with prayer or vow. Animal sacrifices were most effective (pig, sheep, ox), with heart, liver, and kidneys considered best.
  • Human Sacrifice: Generally extraneous but resorted to in major crises. Example: During the Second Punic War (216 BCE), human sacrifice contributed to gladiatorial funeral games.
  • Afterlife Beliefs: Ancestors were meticulously revered. Ideas of the afterlife were vague unless influenced by mystery religions. Both cremation and inhumation were practiced, with a massive reversion to inhumation by the 2nd century CE.

Religious Art

A vast gallery of architecture, sculpture, numismatics, painting, and mosaics illustrates Roman religion.

  • Examples: From primitive statuettes and terra-cotta temple decorations to masterpieces like the Apollo of Veii, Dionysiac mystery paintings at Boscoreale, and the reliefs of Augustus' Ara Pacis.

Conclusion

Despite lacking a comprehensive code of conduct, early Roman religion fostered a feeling of duty and unity through its rituals. The concept of reciprocal understanding between man and god provided security and stimulated the idea of mutual obligations among people.

Roman religion was generally tolerant, unspoiled by orgiastic rites, and its adherents committed few crimes in its name. Most significantly, it provided the firm discipline that gave stability and shape to the mainstream of Western Christianity.
